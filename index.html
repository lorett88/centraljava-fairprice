Common tricks & how to dodge them

None of these are dangerous — Central Java is a very safe place. But a few classic tricks separate tourists from their rupiah every single day.

The unpriced ride

Becak (cycle rickshaw) and street ojek drivers quote nothing, then demand 3–5× the fair price at the end of the ride.

Fix: agree the price out loud before you sit down. Or just use Gojek/Grab — the app locks the price.

"The temple is closed today"

A friendly stranger near a big sight tells you it's closed and offers to take you somewhere "better" — usually a shop paying him commission.

Fix: walk to the official ticket counter and see for yourself. It's almost never closed.

The batik "student exhibition"

Around Yogyakarta & Solo: someone invites you to a one-day-only student art show. The "students" sell mass-printed batik at gallery prices.

Fix: buy batik in proper shops or markets. Real hand-drawn batik tulis starts around Rp 300k — anything cheaper is printed.

Airport SIM markup

Airport counters sell the same tourist SIM for Rp 300–500k that costs Rp 100–175k in any phone shop in town.

Fix: buy your SIM at an official Telkomsel/XL store in the city. Bring your passport for registration.

The ATM conversion trap

ATMs offer to charge your card in euros/pounds "for convenience" (DCC). The hidden exchange margin costs you 3–7%.

Fix: always choose "charge in IDR / without conversion". Use ATMs attached to real banks (BCA, Mandiri, BNI).

The "special tourist menu"

Some warungs near big sights keep a second menu with doubled prices, or serve food with no menu at all and invent the bill.

Fix: eat where prices are written down, or ask "berapa?" (how much?) before ordering. Locals do the same.

Quick answers

Frequently asked questions

Is Central Java cheaper than Bali?
Yes, noticeably. Expect to pay 30–50% less for equivalent food, rooms and transport. A meal that costs Rp 60k in Canggu costs Rp 20–25k in Yogyakarta or Purwokerto — and it's usually better.
Do I need cash, or can I pay by card?
Cash rules outside hotels and malls. Warungs, markets, becak, entrance tickets at smaller sights — all cash. Withdraw from bank-attached ATMs (BCA, Mandiri, BNI), maximum is usually Rp 1.25–2.5 million per withdrawal, and always decline currency conversion.
Is tipping expected?
No. Locals don't tip at warungs or in taxis. Rounding up or leaving Rp 5–10k for great service is appreciated but never expected. For a private driver or guide who spent the whole day with you, Rp 50–100k is a generous thank-you.
When should I haggle, and how hard?
Haggle for becak/ojek rides, souvenirs and market goods — start around half the first quote and settle around 60–70%. Never haggle where prices are printed: restaurants with menus, minimarkets, trains, official ticket counters. Keep it friendly; a smile gets a better price than aggression.
What's the cheapest way to travel between cities in Java?
The train — it's also the most comfortable and scenic. Ekonomi class is very cheap and perfectly fine; eksekutif adds AC-cold comfort and reclining seats for a few euros more. Book via the KAI Access app or 12Go a few days ahead in high season (July–August).
Are these prices accurate?
They're what I and people around me actually pay, updated July 2026. Prices drift a little every year (and spike around Idul Fitri holidays), but the fair-vs-tourist gap stays the same. Big-ticket entrance fees (Borobudur, Prambanan) change by decree — always verify on the official site before you travel.
